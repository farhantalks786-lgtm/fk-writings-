Waqf Amendment Bill; A Reexamination

"Assessing the Legal, Social, and Political Implications of the proposed Changes"

Posted on: Feb 12, 2025

The Waqf Amendment Bill has been a topic of significant debate and discussion in recent times, sparking intense scrutiny and examination from various quarters. Waqf, an Islamic endowment system, plays a vital role in the socio-economic and religious lives of Muslim communities worldwide. However, the proposed amendments have raised important questions about the need for reform, the implications of state intervention, and the potential impact on the autonomy and integrity of Waqf institutions.

The waqf amendment bill was introduced in Lok Sabha on August 8, 2024.It aims to reform the administration and management of waqf properties in India. A permanent dedication or endowment of movable or immovable properties for the benefit of the Muslim community, with the objective of pleasing Allah, this is the beautiful definition of WAQF. In Islamic law, waqf is a charitable act where a person voluntarily dedicates a portion of their wealth or property for the benefit of others. There are a lot of benefits for this system like spiritual rewards, community development, social welfare and economic empowerment.

Waqf properties are managed by Mutawalli(trustee) and waqf board. Waqf is an Islamic endowment of property to be held in trust and used for a charitable or religious purpose. The term ‘WAQF’ comes from the Arabic word which means “confinement” or “prohibition”. The Waqf comprises property or land donated by followers of Islam, and is presently managed by the members of the community. Waqf Boards currently control 8.7 lakh properties spanning 9.4 lakh acres across India, with an estimated value of ₹1.2 lakh crore.

If you take a look into the early beginnings of waqf, you can find that Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) established the first waqf when he dedicated a piece of land for the construction of the mosque of Quba in Medina. The prophet’s companions, such as Abu Bakr and Umar, also dedicated properties for charitable purposes.

And about the expansion and development of waqf properties, During the Rashidun caliphate, Waqf took off as a widespread practice, with caliphs and wealthy Muslims dedicating properties for mosques, schools, and charitable institutions. The Umayyad caliphate saw the rollout of waqf for public utilities, such as water wells and roads. The Abbasid caliphate witnessed a significant growth in waqf, with the establishment of institutions like hospitals, libraries and universities. The Ottoman empire stepped up waqf institutionalization, establishing the ministry of waqf to oversee management. Waqf played a crucial role in financing public works, education and healthcare.

If we discuss the relationship between the Indian subcontinent and the Waqf, there are plenty of histories to say. Muslim rulers in India, such as Delhi Sultanate and Mughal Empire, set aside waqf for mosques, madrasas and charitable institutions.

In conclusion, the Waqf Amendment Bill 2024, despite its intentions to reform and modernize the Waqf administration, falls short in addressing the complexities and concerns of the Muslim community. The proposed amendments threaten the autonomy of Waqf Boards, undermine the rights of minorities, and potentially politicize the management of Waqf properties. Furthermore, the bill's provisions may lead to increased bureaucracy, corruption, and mismanagement.

Rather than streamlining the Waqf administration, the bill may exacerbate existing issues. It is crucial that policymakers reconsider the bill's provisions and engage in meaningful consultations with stakeholders to ensure that any reforms prioritize transparency, accountability, and community benefits.
